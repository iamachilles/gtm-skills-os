# Buying Committee Mapper

Map the complete buying committee for a specific deal type or product sale. B2B deals are won or lost based on how well you navigate the committee. This skill identifies all 6 key roles, defines what each person cares about, anticipates their objections, and provides actionable entry strategies with ready-to-use cold opening lines.

The output is a tactical document that sales reps can use immediately to plan account penetration.

## Inputs

- **Product/service description** (required) — what you sell, who it's for, typical deal size
- **Target company profile** (required) — industry, size, and stage of the companies you're selling to
- **Known persona details** (optional) — any existing persona research or buyer intelligence
- **Typical deal context** (optional) — average deal size, sales cycle length, common entry point

## Steps

### Step 1 — Understand the Product and Deal

Read the provided product description and target company profile. Determine:
- What category does this product fall into?
- What budget does it come from (marketing, sales, IT, operations)?
- What's the typical price point and contract structure?
- Is this a new category purchase or replacing an existing solution?
- How disruptive is the implementation (affects who needs to approve)?

These factors directly determine who sits on the buying committee.

### Step 2 — Research Typical Buying Committees

Use WebSearch to understand how companies in this industry and size range make purchasing decisions:
- Search for buying process insights in the relevant category
- Look at vendor case studies that mention the decision-making process
- Check industry reports on procurement trends
- Research organizational structures at target companies

### Step 3 — Map Each of the 6 Roles

For each role, produce a complete profile:

**Role 1: Economic Buyer**
The person who controls the budget and signs the check. They care about ROI, risk, and strategic alignment.

**Role 2: Champion**
Your internal advocate. They experience the pain directly and are motivated to push for change. They'll sell internally on your behalf if armed with the right materials.

**Role 3: Technical Evaluator**
The person who assesses whether the solution actually works. They care about integration, security, scalability, and implementation effort.

**Role 4: End User**
The daily user of the product. They care about ease of use, workflow fit, and whether it actually makes their job easier or harder.

**Role 5: Blocker/Gatekeeper**
The person who can kill the deal through inaction or objection. Often in procurement, legal, IT security, or a competing department. They don't benefit from the purchase and see it as risk or overhead.

**Role 6: Coach**
An internal ally who gives you intelligence about the process, politics, and competition. They may not have formal authority but they know how things really get done.

For each role, define:
- **Title examples** — 3-5 common titles this person holds in the target company type
- **KPIs they own** — the metrics they're evaluated on
- **What they care about** — their decision criteria for this type of purchase
- **Likely objections** — the 2-3 pushback statements you'll hear
- **Entry strategy** — how to reach and engage this person (channel, timing, approach)
- **Messaging angle** — the value proposition framed for this person's priorities
- **Cold opening line** — one sentence that could start a conversation (email, LinkedIn, or phone)

### Step 4 — Define Entry Sequence

Based on the mapped roles, recommend the optimal deal entry sequence:
- Who to contact first and why
- What information to gather before expanding to other roles
- When to engage the economic buyer (too early = no urgency, too late = no budget)
- How to identify and neutralize blockers early
- When to involve your own leadership (executive alignment)

The sequence should be a numbered list of tactical steps.

### Step 5 — Write Cold Openers

For each role, write one cold opening sentence that:
- References something specific to their role (not generic)
- Connects to a pain point or priority they own
- Creates curiosity without making a product pitch
- Could be used in a cold email, LinkedIn message, or phone call

Bad: "I'd love to show you how we can help your team."
Good: "Noticed you're hiring 3 SDRs. Most sales leaders I work with find that new reps take 6+ months to ramp without a structured sequence system. Curious how you're handling ramp time."

## Quality Checks

- All 6 roles are fully mapped. No role is skipped, even if it's noted as less relevant for this deal type.
- Title examples are realistic for the target company profile. "Chief Digital Transformation Officer" is not a real title at a 50-person startup.
- Cold openers are specific and conversational, not generic sales pitches.
- Objections are real pushback you'd hear in a meeting, not strawman arguments.
- The entry sequence is tactical and specific. "Build relationships" is not a step. "Send a LinkedIn connection request to the VP Sales referencing their recent hiring post" is a step.
- Messaging angles differ across roles. The CFO and the end user should hear different value propositions.
- Deal risk indicators are included so reps can diagnose stalled deals.
