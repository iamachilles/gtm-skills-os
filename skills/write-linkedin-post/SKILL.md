# Write LinkedIn Post

Draft a LinkedIn post optimized for engagement and reach. LinkedIn is a specific medium with its own rules. What works in a blog post or email does not work here. This skill follows a proven framework: hook, body, CTA, with strict copywriting rules that prioritize readability and specificity.

The output includes the post draft, 3 hook variants, CTA options, and a visual format recommendation.

## Inputs

- **Topic** (required) — what the post is about
- **Angle** (required) — how to approach the topic. Choose one:
  - **Storytelling** — personal experience or client story that illustrates a lesson
  - **Documentation** — sharing what you did and what happened (build in public)
  - **Resource** — sharing a framework, template, or tool the audience can use
  - **Education** — teaching a concept or method step by step
  - **Opinion** — taking a stance on a debated topic in the industry
- **Category** (optional) — helps with structure:
  - Case study, lead magnet, tip, opinion, behind-the-scenes, lesson learned, framework, list, question, contrarian take
- **Key message** (optional) — the one takeaway the reader should remember
- **Proof points** (optional) — specific numbers, results, or examples to include
- **Author context** (optional) — who is writing this (role, expertise, perspective)

## Steps

### Step 1 — Understand the Topic and Angle

Read the provided topic, angle, and any supporting context. Identify:
- What is the core insight or lesson?
- Why should the reader care? (What's in it for them?)
- What makes this perspective unique or specific? (Not "outbound is hard" but "cold email reply rates dropped 60% in 18 months and here's what replaced volume")
- What proof or evidence supports the point?

If the topic is too broad, narrow it to one specific idea. A post about "GTM strategy" will perform worse than a post about "the one ICP question that changed how I qualify deals."

### Step 2 — Choose a Category (If Not Provided)

If no category was specified, select the best fit based on the topic and angle:

- **Case study** — "Here's what we did for [client] and what happened"
- **Tip** — "One thing I changed that made a difference"
- **Opinion** — "Most people think X. Here's why I disagree."
- **Framework** — "The 3-step process I use for [task]"
- **List** — "5 things I learned from [experience]"
- **Behind-the-scenes** — "Here's what actually happened when we tried [thing]"
- **Question** — "I've been thinking about [topic]. What's your take?"
- **Contrarian take** — "Everyone says [common belief]. But the data shows [opposite]."
- **Lesson learned** — "I made this mistake. Here's what I'd do differently."
- **Lead magnet** — "I built [resource]. Here's how to get it."

### Step 3 — Write the Hook (First 2 Lines)

The hook is the most critical element. It determines whether anyone reads the rest. LinkedIn shows approximately the first 2 lines before the "see more" button. Those 2 lines must create enough curiosity to earn the click.

**Hook formulas that work:**
- **Contrarian opener:** "Stop [common practice]. It's [why it doesn't work]."
- **Specific result:** "We [did X] and [result with number]."
- **Pattern interrupt:** "[Unexpected statement that challenges assumptions]."
- **Question:** "What if [reframe of common problem]?"
- **Story opener:** "Last week, a [persona] told me something that changed how I think about [topic]."
- **List tease:** "3 [things] I wish I knew before [experience]."

**Hook rules:**
- Be specific. Numbers outperform vague claims.
- Create an information gap. The reader needs to expand to get the answer.
- No clickbait. The post must deliver on what the hook promises.
- Under 20 words for the first line.

Write 3 hook variants so the best one can be selected.

### Step 4 — Write the Body

The body delivers on the hook's promise. Follow these copywriting rules:

**Structure rules:**
- One idea per paragraph.
- One verb per sentence (when possible). Keep sentences short and punchy.
- Short paragraphs (1-3 lines max). Wall of text kills engagement.
- Use line breaks liberally. White space is your friend on LinkedIn.
- Lists and numbered steps increase readability.

**Content rules:**
- Specific numbers over vague claims. "Increased reply rate from 2% to 8%" beats "significantly improved results."
- First person. Write as yourself, not as a brand.
- Show, don't tell. Don't say "it's important to be specific." Show what specific looks like with an example.
- No filler sentences. Every sentence must advance the point or provide evidence. Delete anything that's just connecting tissue.
- No consulting jargon. "Leverage synergies to optimize pipeline velocity" makes people scroll past. "We changed one thing in the email and doubled the replies" makes people stop.

**Forbidden elements:**
- No em dashes. Use periods, commas, or line breaks instead.
- No hashtag stuffing. 3 relevant hashtags maximum, placed at the end.
- No "let me explain" or "here's the thing" or "think about it this way" filler phrases.
- No rhetorical questions in the body (save questions for hooks or CTAs).
- No emoji overload. Zero to two emojis maximum, and only if they add meaning.

**Post length:**
- Aim for 150-250 words. Long enough to deliver value, short enough to hold attention.
- Lists and frameworks can go longer (up to 300 words) because they're scannable.
- Storytelling posts earn their length through narrative tension.

### Step 5 — Write the CTA

Every post needs a call to action. It can be soft or direct:

**Soft CTAs (engagement-focused):**
- "What's been your experience with [topic]?"
- "Agree or disagree?"
- "What would you add to this list?"

**Direct CTAs (lead-focused):**
- "If you're dealing with [problem], DM me [keyword] and I'll send you [resource]."
- "I'm writing a guide on [topic]. Drop a comment if you want early access."
- "Link to [resource] in the comments."

**CTA rules:**
- One CTA per post. Not three.
- Match the CTA to the post type. A storytelling post works better with a soft CTA. A resource post works better with a direct CTA.
- Make it low-friction. "DM me one word" is easier than "fill out this form."

Write 2-3 CTA options so the best one can be selected.

### Step 6 — Suggest a Visual Format

Recommend a visual format to pair with the post:

- **Text only** — works for opinions, short takes, questions
- **Single image** — works for frameworks, diagrams, before/after comparisons
- **Carousel** — works for step-by-step guides, lists, educational content (best performing format for reach)
- **Screenshot** — works for data, results, real examples
- **Video** — works for storytelling, demonstrations, behind-the-scenes
- **Document/PDF** — works for lead magnets and detailed frameworks

Include a brief description of what the visual should contain.

## Quality Checks

- The hook creates genuine curiosity. Read just the first 2 lines. Would you click "see more"?
- No em dashes anywhere in the post.
- No consulting jargon. Read it aloud. Does it sound like a real person talking?
- Every sentence adds value. Delete any sentence and check if the post still makes sense. If it reads better without the sentence, cut it.
- Specific numbers are used wherever possible. "Many companies" should be "47% of Series B SaaS companies" (or whatever the actual data shows).
- The post is written in first person.
- The CTA matches the post type and asks for one thing.
- The post is 150-300 words (check the count).
- Three hook variants are provided, each using a different formula.
- The visual recommendation matches the content type.
- No hashtag stuffing. Maximum 3 hashtags.
- The post delivers on the hook's promise. No bait-and-switch.
